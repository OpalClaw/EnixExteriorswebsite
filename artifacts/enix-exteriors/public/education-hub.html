CONTRACTOR GUIDE
How to Choose a Roofing Contractor in Tennessee
8 min read

Choosing the right roofing contractor is one of the most important decisions you'll make as a Tennessee property owner. The wrong choice can cost you thousands in subpar work, voided warranties, and repeated repairs. Here's what you need to know before signing any contract.

1. Verify Licensing and Insurance

In Tennessee, roofing contractors should carry general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage. Always request certificates of insurance directly from the insurer — not just a copy from the contractor. If a worker is injured on your property without proper coverage, you could be held liable.

  • General liability: Covers property damage caused during work
  • Workers' comp: Covers injuries to workers on your property
  • Contractor's license: Verify through the Tennessee Department of Commerce & Insurance

2. Check Their Local Reputation

After major storms, out-of-state contractors flood Tennessee communities. While some are reputable, many are "storm chasers" who collect deposits and disappear. Always choose a contractor with a verifiable local presence.

  • Look for a physical address — not a P.O. box
  • Check Google reviews, BBB ratings, and Angi listings
  • Ask for references from completed jobs in your area
  • Verify how long they've operated in Tennessee

3. Get Multiple Written Estimates

Never accept a verbal quote. A proper written estimate should include:

  • Specific materials (manufacturer, product line, color)
  • Scope of work (tear-off, decking repairs, flashing replacement)
  • Timeline for completion
  • Warranty details for both labor and materials
  • Total price with no hidden fees

4. Understand Warranty Coverage

There are two types of roofing warranties: manufacturer warranties covering defective materials, and contractor warranties covering workmanship. The best contractors offer manufacturer-certified installation, which provides enhanced warranty coverage that a non-certified installer cannot provide.

5. Never Pay Full Price Upfront

A reputable contractor will request a deposit (typically 10–30%) to order materials, with the balance due upon completion. Paying in full before work begins is a major red flag. Also avoid contractors who pressure you to sign immediately or claim the offer expires today.

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Requires full payment before starting
  • No physical address or local presence
  • Won't provide proof of insurance
  • Unusually low bid (often means inferior materials)
  • High-pressure sales tactics
  • Asks you to sign insurance documents on their behalf

Bottom line: A reputable Tennessee roofing contractor will be transparent, licensed, insured, and happy to provide references. Take your time, do your research, and never let urgency cloud your judgment.

TPO vs. Modified Bitumen: Which Commercial Roof is Right for You?
7 min read

If you own or manage a commercial building in Tennessee, chances are you're dealing with a flat or low-slope roof. The two most popular systems for these roofs are TPO (Thermoplastic Polyolefin) and Modified Bitumen. Understanding the differences helps you make the right investment for your building.

What is TPO Roofing?

TPO is a single-ply roofing membrane made from synthetic rubber. It comes in rolls that are heat-welded together on-site, creating a seamless waterproof surface. TPO is currently the most popular commercial roofing material in the United States.

TPO Advantages

  • Energy efficiency: White TPO reflects up to 90% of UV rays, reducing cooling costs — significant in Tennessee summers
  • Cost-effective: Generally less expensive than modified bitumen
  • Lightweight: Puts less stress on your building structure
  • Strong seams: Heat-welded seams are stronger than the membrane itself
  • Low maintenance: Easy to inspect and repair

TPO Disadvantages

  • Relatively newer technology — long-term performance data is still being established
  • Quality varies significantly between manufacturers
  • Requires experienced installers for proper heat-welding

What is Modified Bitumen?

Modified bitumen is an asphalt-based roofing system reinforced with polyester or fiberglass. It's applied in multiple layers (typically 2–4) using heat torching, cold adhesive, or self-adhesion. Modified bitumen has been widely used since the 1970s and has an excellent long-term track record.

Modified Bitumen Advantages

  • Proven track record: Decades of performance data in diverse climates
  • Multi-layer protection: Redundancy means better leak resistance
  • Excellent impact resistance: Handles hail and foot traffic better than TPO
  • Easy repair: Patches are straightforward and durable

Modified Bitumen Disadvantages

  • Heavier than TPO, requiring adequate structural support
  • Dark-colored standard options absorb more heat (though white-coated versions exist)
  • Generally higher material and installation cost than TPO

Which Should You Choose?

For most commercial buildings in Tennessee, TPO is the preferred choice due to its energy efficiency, cost-effectiveness, and strong performance in our hot summers. However, for buildings with heavy foot traffic, extreme weather exposure, or structural concerns about weight, modified bitumen may be the better option.

Asphalt Shingles vs. Metal Roofing: A Complete Homeowner's Guide
9 min read

If you're replacing your Tennessee home's roof, the two most popular material choices are asphalt shingles and metal roofing. Both have strong advantages, and the right choice depends on your budget, home style, and long-term plans.

Asphalt Shingles: The Tennessee Standard

Asphalt shingles account for roughly 80% of residential roofing in the United States. Modern architectural (laminated) shingles are dramatically improved over older 3-tab styles, offering better aesthetics, wind resistance, and longevity.

Pros of Asphalt Shingles

  • Affordability: Typically 40–60% less expensive upfront than metal
  • Wide selection: Hundreds of colors and styles to match any home
  • Easy repair: Individual shingles can be replaced without disturbing the rest
  • Widely available: Any qualified roofing contractor can install them
  • Lifespan: Quality architectural shingles last 25–40 years with proper maintenance

Cons of Asphalt Shingles

  • Shorter lifespan than metal (25–40 years vs. 40–70+ years)
  • More susceptible to algae growth in Tennessee's humid climate
  • Absorbs more heat than metal, increasing cooling costs
  • Granule loss over time reduces protection

Metal Roofing: The Long-Term Investment

Standing seam metal roofing has surged in popularity in Tennessee over the past decade. While the upfront cost is higher, metal roofs often pay for themselves over time through longevity and energy savings.

Pros of Metal Roofing

  • Longevity: A properly installed metal roof can last 40–70+ years
  • Energy efficiency: Reflective coatings can reduce cooling costs by 10–25%
  • Extreme weather resistance: Handles high winds (120+ mph rated panels), hail, and heavy snow
  • Low maintenance: No shingle granule loss, no algae, minimal upkeep
  • Environmentally friendly: Often made from recycled content and fully recyclable at end of life

Cons of Metal Roofing

  • Higher upfront cost (typically 2–3x the cost of asphalt)
  • Requires specialized installation — not all roofers are qualified
  • Can be noisier during heavy rain (though proper underlayment minimizes this)
  • Dents from large hail are possible, though panels can be replaced individually

Cost Comparison for Tennessee Homeowners

For a typical 2,000 sq ft Tennessee home:

  • Asphalt shingles: $8,000 – $14,000 installed
  • Standing seam metal: $18,000 – $32,000 installed

While the price difference seems significant, consider the lifespan. A homeowner who replaces asphalt shingles twice over 50 years may spend more total than a homeowner who installs a metal roof once.

Our Recommendation

If you plan to stay in your home long-term and want the lowest maintenance option, metal roofing is an excellent investment in Tennessee. If budget is the primary concern or you may sell within 10–15 years, high-quality architectural shingles are the smart, cost-effective choice. What to Do Immediately After Storm Damage to Your Roof
6 min read

A severe storm just rolled through your area. You notice ceiling stains, missing shingles, or damage to your gutters. The next few hours are critical. Here's exactly what to do — and what to avoid — after storm damage to your Tennessee property.

Step 1: Stay Safe First

Before inspecting anything, ensure your family is safe. Do not go onto your roof — especially when it's wet or damaged. Walking on a compromised roof can make damage worse and is extremely dangerous. Observe from the ground only.

Step 2: Document Everything Immediately

Before any cleanup or temporary repairs, photograph and video everything you can see:

  • Damaged shingles, gutters, siding, and any debris
  • Interior ceiling stains or wet spots
  • Date and time stamps on all photos
  • Wide shots showing the extent and location of damage

This documentation is critical for your insurance claim. Never skip this step.

Step 3: Call a Professional Roofer (Not Your Insurance Company First)

This surprises many homeowners, but calling a reputable roofing contractor before your insurance company is often beneficial. An experienced roofer can:

  • Perform a free professional damage assessment
  • Document damage that's not visible from the ground
  • Perform emergency tarping to prevent further water damage
  • Provide a repair estimate that strengthens your insurance claim

Step 4: File Your Insurance Claim

Once you have professional documentation, contact your homeowner's insurance company. Provide them with:

  • All photos and videos you captured
  • The contractor's written damage assessment
  • Date of the storm (you can reference local weather records)

Step 5: Emergency Tarping if Needed

If your roof has active leaks or significant openings, emergency tarping protects your home's interior while you wait for the adjuster's visit. A reputable roofer will handle this professionally and document it for your claim.

Step 6: Meet the Insurance Adjuster

When your adjuster arrives, have your roofing contractor present if possible. Your contractor can point out all damage areas and ensure nothing is missed. Adjusters sometimes overlook secondary damage areas that significantly add to your claim value.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Never sign over your insurance rights to a contractor (Assignment of Benefits)
  • Don't wait weeks to file — most policies have deadlines
  • Don't hire the first contractor who knocks on your door after a storm
  • Don't make permanent repairs before the adjuster inspects

Signs Your Roof Needs Replacement vs. Repair
6 min read

One of the most common questions Tennessee homeowners ask is: "Do I need a full roof replacement or just a repair?" The answer depends on the age of your roof, the extent of damage, and several other factors. Here's how to tell the difference.

Signs You Probably Need a Repair (Not a Replacement)

  • Isolated damage in a small area: A few missing or damaged shingles that represent less than 25% of your roof
  • Young roof: If your roof is less than 10 years old and otherwise in good shape
  • Isolated leak: A single leak traced to a specific flashing failure or small area
  • Recent storm damage to a previously healthy roof: Insurance may cover targeted repairs

Signs You Probably Need a Full Replacement

  • Age: Asphalt shingle roofs over 20–25 years old should be inspected carefully. Most should be replaced before 30 years.
  • Widespread granule loss: Significant granules in your gutters mean shingles are at end of life
  • Multiple areas of damage: When more than 25–30% of the roof is damaged or deteriorated
  • Sagging or soft spots: Indicates decking damage that requires significant structural work
  • Multiple past repairs: If you've repaired repeatedly and leaks keep returning, replacement is more cost-effective
  • Curling, cupping, or cracking shingles: Throughout the entire roof, not just in one spot
  • Interior daylight visible: You can see light through the attic — serious structural concern
  • Moss or algae throughout: Widespread organic growth accelerates shingle deterioration

The 50% Rule

Many roofing professionals use this guideline: if repairs would cost more than 50% of replacement cost, replacement is the smarter long-term investment. You get a fresh start, new warranty coverage, and peace of mind for 25+ years.

Gutter Maintenance 101: Protecting Your Tennessee Home
5 min read

Most Tennessee homeowners don't think much about their gutters until something goes seriously wrong. But clogged, damaged, or improperly installed gutters can cause foundation damage, basement flooding, siding rot, and even roof damage — all expensive problems that start with a simple oversight.

Why Gutters Matter

Gutters have one job: channel rainwater away from your home's foundation. Tennessee receives an average of 50+ inches of rain per year — significantly above the national average. Without properly functioning gutters, all that water runs directly down your walls and pools around your foundation.

How Often Should You Clean Your Gutters?

The general recommendation for Tennessee homeowners:

  • Fall: After leaves have fully dropped (October/November)
  • Spring: After spring storms and pollen season (March/April)
  • Additional cleaning if you have overhanging trees

Signs Your Gutters Need Attention

  • Water overflowing during rain (clog)
  • Gutters pulling away from the fascia (improper slope or damaged hangers)
  • Visible sagging in gutter runs
  • Standing water in gutters after rain (improper slope)
  • Rust stains or holes
  • Water stains on siding below gutters
  • Erosion in mulch beds directly below gutters

Seamless Gutters vs. Sectional Gutters

Sectional gutters are factory-made in standard lengths and joined with connectors on-site. The connectors are the weak points — they require sealant that can fail over time, creating leaks.

Seamless gutters are custom-formed on-site from a single continuous piece of aluminum, with seams only at corners and downspout connections. They're more expensive upfront but require less maintenance and last longer. Enix Exteriors installs seamless aluminum gutters in custom sizes and colors.

Leaf Guards: Worth the Investment?

For homes with significant tree coverage, leaf guard systems can dramatically reduce cleaning frequency. Quality micro-mesh guards allow water through while blocking debris. The payback comes quickly when you factor in cleaning costs and the risk of ladder accidents from DIY cleaning.

Navigating Insurance Claims for Roof Storm Damage in Tennessee
7 min read

Tennessee is no stranger to severe weather. From tornado outbreaks to hail storms and powerful thunderstorms, your roof takes a beating year after year. When significant damage occurs, knowing how to navigate the insurance claim process can mean the difference between a full replacement and a denied claim.

Is Storm Damage Covered?

Standard homeowner's insurance policies in Tennessee cover sudden and accidental damage from storms — including hail, wind, tornadoes, and lightning. What is generally not covered includes:

  • Normal wear and tear or deterioration
  • Damage from lack of maintenance
  • Pre-existing damage
  • Flooding (requires separate flood insurance)

Actual Cash Value vs. Replacement Cost Value

This distinction in your policy dramatically affects your payout:

  • Actual Cash Value (ACV): Pays you what your roof is worth today, accounting for depreciation. A 15-year-old roof may receive significantly less than replacement cost.
  • Replacement Cost Value (RCV): Pays to replace your roof with like-kind materials at current prices, regardless of age. This is the superior coverage and worth the additional premium.

The Claims Process Step by Step

  • Get a professional inspection from a licensed contractor before filing
  • File your claim promptly — most policies have a deadline (often 1 year from the storm event)
  • Document the storm date using weather history records
  • Be present when the adjuster inspects your property
  • Have your contractor present to point out all damage
  • Review the adjuster's estimate carefully for missed items
  • Request a supplement if items were overlooked

Working with a Roofing Contractor During Claims

A reputable roofing contractor will assist you through the claims process without requiring you to sign over your insurance rights. Be wary of Assignment of Benefits (AOB) agreements that transfer control of your claim to the contractor — this is a red flag.

Understanding Roof Warranties: What Every Tennessee Property Owner Should Know
6 min read

A roofing warranty is only as valuable as the coverage it provides and the contractor who stands behind it. Many property owners are surprised after a roofing problem to discover their warranty doesn't cover what they thought it did. Here's what you need to know.

The Two Types of Roofing Warranties

1. Manufacturer Warranties

Material warranties are provided by the shingle or membrane manufacturer (GAF, Owens Corning, CertainTeed, Firestone, etc.). They cover defects in the manufacturing of materials — not installation errors or storm damage. Standard manufacturer warranties range from 25 to 50 years on residential shingles.

2. Contractor (Workmanship) Warranties

Provided by your roofing contractor, workmanship warranties cover errors in installation — improper nailing, insufficient overlap, missing flashing, etc. These vary widely: from 1 year with discount contractors to 10+ years with established, reputable companies.

Enhanced (System) Warranties

The best warranty coverage is available when your contractor is certified to install a manufacturer's complete roofing system. GAF's Golden Pledge warranty, for example, covers materials AND workmanship for up to 25 years — but only a GAF Master Elite contractor can offer it.

This is why choosing a manufacturer-certified contractor matters: you get warranty protection that a non-certified competitor literally cannot provide.

What Voids a Warranty

  • Installation by a non-certified contractor
  • Improper attic ventilation
  • Walking on the roof improperly
  • Adding solar panels or HVAC equipment without proper flashing
  • Failure to report damage promptly
  • Mixing different manufacturer's products in the same system

Questions to Ask Your Contractor

  • Are you certified by the shingle manufacturer?
  • What does your workmanship warranty cover, and for how long?
  • Is the warranty transferable if I sell my home?
  • What voids the warranty?
  • Will you provide warranty documentation in writing?
